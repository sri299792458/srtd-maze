Yes. The paper waiting to be written is **not** “apply FFT to robot actions.” It is deeper:

# Trajectory Diffusion Is Spectral Skill Autoregression

The missing synthesis is this: **robot trajectories have a predictable spectral envelope, but task success is often determined by sparse spectral residuals.** Diffusion policies already traverse this spectrum from low-frequency plan to high-frequency execution. A seminal robotics paper would make that structure explicit and use it to learn, segment, transfer, and denoise behavior in trajectory space.

Sander Dieleman’s key point is that diffusion on images behaves like **approximate autoregression in frequency space**: natural images have power-law spectra, Gaussian noise is spectrally flat, and adding noise drowns high frequencies before low frequencies, so reverse diffusion reconstructs coarse-to-fine structure. He explicitly frames this as “approximate spectral autoregression,” while noting that the noise-frequency mapping is smooth and distributional rather than a hard per-sample rule. ([Sander Dieleman][1])

Hou and Zhang’s spectral residual paper supplies the missing “saliency” operator: do not merely observe that natural signals have a predictable spectral envelope; subtract that envelope and call what remains **innovation**. Their model decomposes image information into prior knowledge plus innovation, estimates the smooth log-spectrum, subtracts it to obtain the spectral residual, and inverse-transforms the residual plus phase to get a spatial saliency map of proto-objects.   

The Ambient Diffusion Policy paper already takes the first robotics step: robot action data exhibits a spectral power law; diffusion policies exhibit a global-to-local hierarchy; Gaussian noise acts like a high-frequency mask; and suboptimal robot data can be used only at diffusion times where it agrees with target data.   

The next paper should generalize this from **noise-dependent data usage** to **spectral-residual trajectory learning**.

## The core thesis

A robot trajectory is like a natural image in the following sense: most of its energy is boring, smooth, predictable, and power-law distributed. But the important parts are not uniformly distributed. They are **spectral surprises**: contact onset, grasp closure, constraint activation, loss of sticking, snap-through, insertion alignment, regrasp, mode switch, collision avoidance, force correction, recovery after slip.

In images, spectral residuals reveal proto-objects. In trajectories, spectral residuals should reveal **proto-skills** or **proto-events**.

The slogan:

> **A trajectory is a power-law envelope plus sparse task-critical residuals. Diffusion over trajectories is autoregression over this envelope and its residuals.**

Ambient Diffusion Policy says: high noise learns global plan, low noise learns local primitives. The bigger move is to ask: **which spectral bands, timesteps, joints, objects, and contact modes actually contain the innovation?** The answer should drive the policy architecture, loss, data mixture, diffusion schedule, and evaluation.

## The object of study: trajectory saliency

Let a trajectory be

[
x_{0:T} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d},
]

where (d) may include joint positions, end-effector pose increments, gripper state, object poses, force/torque, tactile features, or latent action tokens.

Choose an orthonormal trajectory basis (\Psi). The simplest version is temporal Fourier:

[
\hat{x}(\omega) = \mathcal{F}_t x.
]

The more serious version is a product basis:

[
\Psi = \Psi_{\text{time}} \otimes \Psi_{\text{body}} \otimes \Psi_{\text{object}},
]

where (\Psi_{\text{time}}) can be Fourier, DCT, STFT, or wavelet; (\Psi_{\text{body}}) can be a graph Fourier basis on the robot kinematic tree; and (\Psi_{\text{object}}) can be a graph basis over object/contact relations. For SE(3), use tangent-space coordinates in (\mathfrak{se}(3)), with careful unwrapping and normalization.

Then define the **conditional spectral envelope**:

[
A_\theta(\omega \mid o, g, e)
= \mathbb{E}_\theta[\log(|\hat{x}(\omega)|+\epsilon) \mid \text{observation } o, \text{ goal } g, \text{ embodiment } e].
]

The **trajectory spectral residual** is

[
R_x(\omega)
===========

## \log(|\hat{x}(\omega)|+\epsilon)

A_\theta(\omega \mid o,g,e).
]

Then reconstruct a trajectory-space saliency map using the residual magnitude and original phase:

[
S_x(t,j)
========

\left|
\Psi^{-1}
\left(
\exp(R_x(\omega)) \cdot e^{i \phi_x(\omega)}
\right)
\right|^2_{t,j}.
]

This is the direct analogue of spectral residual saliency, but the output is no longer a heatmap over pixels. It is a heatmap over **time × degree-of-freedom × object/contact channel**.

A high value of (S_x(t,j)) should mean: “something non-generic and behaviorally important is happening here.”

That is the first publishable result: **trajectory saliency without labels**.

## What the method should do

Call it **Spectral Residual Trajectory Diffusion**.

It should have four coupled modules.

First, a **spectral envelope model** learns the boring part of robot motion: smoothness, power-law decay, embodiment-specific actuation bandwidth, task-conditioned low-frequency structure.

Second, a **residual saliency model** identifies the surprising parts: contacts, switches, high-curvature corrections, discontinuities, local constraint satisfaction, recovery maneuvers.

Third, a **frequency-aware diffusion process** no longer uses isotropic action noise as a black box. In spectral coordinates:

[
\hat{x}_t(\omega)
=================

\alpha_t(\omega)\hat{x}*0(\omega)
+
\sigma_t(\omega)\epsilon*\omega.
]

The detectable bands at diffusion time (t) are those whose spectral SNR exceeds a threshold:

[
\text{SNR}_t(\omega)
====================

\frac{\alpha_t(\omega)^2 P_\theta(\omega)}
{\sigma_t(\omega)^2}.
]

This turns diffusion time into an interpretable curriculum: global object motion and route topology first, grasp/contact/micro-corrections later.

Fourth, a **residual-gated training loss** focuses model capacity where the innovation is:

[
\mathcal{L}
===========

\sum_{\omega}
w(\omega, x, o)
\left|
\hat{x}_{0,\theta}(\omega)
--------------------------

\hat{x}_0(\omega)
\right|^2,
]

where (w) increases on bands/times with high residual saliency and decreases on generic smooth bands that the model already captures.

The important shift is this: diffusion policy becomes less like “denoise all actions equally” and more like **autoregressively explain the predictable envelope, then spend computation on the residuals that matter**.

## The killer generalization of Ambient Diffusion Policy

Ambient Diffusion Policy uses scalar intervals (t \in [0,t_{\max}) \cup (t_{\min},T]) to decide when suboptimal data may contribute. That is already powerful. But it is still coarse. The next paper should replace scalar timestep gates with **spectral compatibility masks**.

For each auxiliary dataset (D_q), learn where it agrees with the target dataset (D_p):

[
M_q(\omega, t, c)
\in
{0,1},
]

where (c) is context: task, embodiment, object, environment, contact regime.

Then train on auxiliary data only when its spectral content is useful:

[
\mathcal{L}_{q}
===============

\sum_{\omega,t}
M_q(\omega,t,c)
\left|
\hat{x}_{0,\theta}^{q}(\omega,t)
--------------------------------

\hat{x}_0^q(\omega)
\right|^2.
]

This would strictly generalize Ambient’s idea. Ambient says: “Use suboptimal data at high noise if low-frequency plan agrees, and at low noise if local primitives agree.” The spectral-residual version says: “Use each dataset only for the bands and events where its envelope or residual matches the target.”

That unlocks very concrete cases:

A sampling-based planner may be useful for low-frequency maze topology but harmful for high-frequency smoothness.

A simulator may be useful for global object displacement but harmful for contact-rich microdynamics.

Human video may be useful for object-space low-frequency intent but useless for robot joint-space residuals.

Failed demonstrations may contain useful prefixes, grasps, recoveries, or contact events even if the final outcome is bad.

Cross-embodiment data may share object-space spectral structure while disagreeing in embodiment-space high-frequency bands.

This is a more fine-grained answer to the data-mixture problem than filtering, reweighting, or scalar (t_{\min}) sweeps. The Ambient paper itself shows that scalar noise-dependent usage can outperform co-training and filtering, including in OXE-scale experiments; the spectral mask version is the natural next step. 

## The conceptual contribution: proto-skills

The saliency paper talks about proto-objects: candidates that pop out before recognition. The robotics analogue is **proto-skills**.

A proto-skill is not yet a symbolic skill like “grasp mug” or “insert peg.” It is a trajectory event that becomes salient because it violates the smooth predictable background of motion.

Examples:

The gripper closing instant.

The first nonzero contact wrench.

The moment a pushed object transitions from sticking to sliding.

The short corrective twist before insertion.

The reorientation pulse before placing a block.

The recovery movement after a failed grasp.

The spectral residual map should detect these without labels. Then the policy can use them as latent anchors.

That would bridge low-level imitation and high-level task structure without hand-designed skill boundaries.

## The paper’s most important empirical figure

The figure that would make people pay attention is a trajectory analogue of a visual saliency map.

Show a manipulation trajectory with synchronized video, action, force, and gripper state.

Below it, show:

1. Raw trajectory.

3. Spectral residual.
4. Inverse residual saliency over time and joints.

The punchline should be that residual saliency spikes at behaviorally meaningful events, even without event labels.

That figure would do for robot trajectories what Hou and Zhang’s saliency maps did for images.

## Experiments that would make it seminal

The first experiment should establish the **trajectory spectral law** beyond action chunks. Ambient already shows power-law structure in robot action data across multiple datasets. The next paper should test richer signals: joint trajectories, end-effector trajectories, object poses, force/torque, tactile streams, visual latent trajectories, and graph-Fourier modes over the kinematic tree. 

The second experiment should test **residual saliency as event discovery**. Use tasks with labeled or automatically detectable events: grasp closure, contact onset, peg insertion, tool contact, drawer opening, object release, slip. Compare against acceleration peaks, jerk, force thresholds, learned segmentation, and attention maps.

The third experiment should test **residual-gated diffusion policy**. Compare against Diffusion Policy, Ambient Diffusion Policy, data filtering, co-training, and hierarchical skill baselines. Use sparse expert data plus large auxiliary data. The method should win on success, smoothness, contact precision, and sample efficiency.

The fourth experiment should test **spectral data routing**. Construct auxiliary datasets where different bands are useful or harmful. For example: RRT paths are globally useful but locally jittery; simulation is globally useful but contact-locally biased; wrong-task pick-place data has useful grasp primitives but wrong logic. Ambient already demonstrates versions of these; the new paper should show that spectral masks choose the right bands automatically.  

The fifth experiment should test **long-horizon robustness**. Spectral residuals should matter most when success depends on a few decisive micro-events inside long smooth behavior: tower building, assembly, insertion, cable routing, cloth manipulation, tool use, food prep.

## The theory section

A clean theoretical model could be:

[
x = z + r,
]

where (z) is a stationary smooth trajectory process with power-law spectrum,

[
P_z(\omega) \propto |\omega|^{-\alpha},
]

and (r) is a sparse residual process localized in time, frequency, or graph-frequency.

Then prove four things.

First: **noise-as-bandlimiting**. Under a power-law envelope, Gaussian noise hides high-frequency trajectory components before low-frequency components, so diffusion induces spectral autoregression.

Second: **residual recoverability**. If the envelope is locally smooth in log-spectrum and the residual is sparse or nonstationary, then subtracting a local or learned spectral envelope recovers the support of task-relevant innovations.

Third: **locality of denoising**. Ambient already proves a version where spectral power laws imply locality of the optimal denoiser at low noise. The new theory should extend this to multi-channel trajectory bases and graph-time product spaces. 

Fourth: **unbiased auxiliary data use**. If (D_q) matches (D_p) on a spectral subset (\Omega) but differs outside it, then training on (D_q) with mask (M_q(\Omega)=1) reduces variance without introducing bias on the target denoiser for those bands.

That last theorem would be the mathematical heart of the paper.

## Why this is more than a better diffusion policy

The deeper contribution is a new **coordinate system for robot learning**.

Right now, robot learning often treats trajectories as sequences of action vectors. But sequence time is not the right primitive. The physically meaningful decomposition is something like:

low temporal frequency: intent, route, object displacement, task phase;

mid frequency: approach geometry, obstacle avoidance, grasp alignment, pushing mode;

high frequency: contact correction, compliance, actuator jitter, slip response, force closure;

graph frequency over body: whole-arm coordination versus distal wrist/finger corrections;

object/contact frequency: global scene rearrangement versus local contact-mode transition.

A good robot policy should know which level it is modeling at each denoising step.

That is the “seminal” part: **robot trajectories become natural signals with geometry, spectra, residuals, and saliency.**

## The dangerous pitfalls

The paper should not pretend Fourier alone solves robotics. Pure Fourier assumes stationarity; manipulation is full of phase changes and contact discontinuities. The right implementation likely needs STFTs, wavelets, event-aligned time normalization, and graph spectral bases.

It should not discard phase. The spectral residual saliency method preserves phase when inverse-transforming the residual, which is essential for localization. A trajectory version must also preserve phase or it will know “what frequency” but not “when the event happened.” 

It should not smooth away contact. In many robot tasks, the “ugly” high-frequency component is not noise; it is the task. Peg insertion, scraping, wiping, dynamic catching, and slip recovery may live in precisely the bands a naive smoothness prior would suppress.

It should not confuse action-space and observation-space shifts. Ambient notes that its strong results address action-space distribution shift, while observation-space shift remains open. A trajectory-residual paper should either stay honest about that boundary or extend the residual idea to visual/tactile latent trajectories. 

## A strong paper title and abstract

**Title:**
**Trajectory Diffusion Is Spectral Skill Autoregression**

**Abstract sketch:**
Robot trajectories exhibit strong spectral regularities, yet task success is often determined by sparse deviations from these regularities: contacts, mode switches, corrections, and releases. Inspired by spectral autoregression in diffusion models and spectral residual saliency in vision, we introduce Spectral Residual Trajectory Diffusion, a framework that decomposes robot behavior into a predictable spectral envelope and task-critical residuals. We show that residuals localize proto-skill events, that diffusion policies denoise trajectories in a low-to-high frequency hierarchy, and that auxiliary datasets can be routed by spectral compatibility rather than global dataset weights. Across contact-rich manipulation, motion planning, and cross-embodiment imitation, our method improves sample efficiency, smoothness, event precision, and robustness to suboptimal data.

## The one-sentence contribution

**The paper should show that the right unit of robot imitation is not the timestep, the action chunk, or the demonstration, but the spectrally localized residual event.**
